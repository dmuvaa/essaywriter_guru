Ang Pobanz RE: Discussion 2 – Week 6 – Initial Question COLLAPSE Necessary Conditions for Obedience Fiske (2014) defines obedience as “the influence of authority demands on subordinates” (p. 530). In order for obedience to occur, certain conditions are necessary. Milgram found that research participants would deliver increasingly painful shocks to another participant if they were prompted to do so in the name of science. The Milgram experiment revealed that having an authority figure in the same room increased the likelihood of the participant delivering higher voltage shocks (BBC, n.d.). However, if the participant being shocked sat in the same room as the participant delivering the shock, obedience decreased. As an aside, this may help explain why online abuse and bullying can appear so dramatically different from face-to-face bullying. Being hidden and/or anonymous makes being the “bad guy” easier. Additionally, obedience was proportional to the immediacy of the victim; the more immediate the victim, the greater the decrease in obedience of the participant. Also, if a peer rebels, observers are more likely to refuse to obey as well particularly if the task makes them uncomfortable (Fiske, 2014). Other things to factor in when considering conditions for obedience include individual differences, personality variables, type of society (collectivist vs. individualistic), time frame (more conservative periods in history vs. more experimental or questioning), and core motives for allocating control – in this case to an authority figure, belonging (by being cooperative and “good”), and understanding (relying on an authority figure to know what is best) (Fiske, 2014). Obedience as Necessary When I think of necessary obedience I think of a military unit engaged in combat. The rules of a platoon are made very clear to every member and there is a clear expectation that each soldier follows orders, obey the chain of command, and behave in ways consistent with their extensive training. A high degree of obedience in this case is what helps keep military personnel alive and helps to achieve given combat objectives. When soldiers do not obey, it can cost lives. Obedience as Harmful Obedience can be harmful in a number of ways. One historical example is found in the Nazi regime, which was one of the seminal motivations for Milgram’s experiments. As a person who identified as Jewish, he wanted to better understand how seemingly ordinary people could carry out such atrocities as those experienced by the Jewish people and Jewish sympathizers during the Holocaust (Benjamin & Simpson, 2009; Fiske, 2014). The same authority that worked for Milgram’s actors in his experiments were at work in the Nazi regime. Because of what some might term “blind” obedience to authority, an entire army of loyalists to the Nazi regime carried out orders of genocide. Additionally, widespread acceptance of Hitler’s propaganda and persuasion, and the observance of others following orders helped the rationalization and justification of discrimination and extermination unfold.
